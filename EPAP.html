Logo design — Adobe Illustrator & Figma — 2022
Brief:

Design a logo for European Parental Alienation Practice (EPAP).
What is EPAP?:

EPAP is a collaboration between Parental Alienation Europe and the Institute of Family Therapy Malta to contribute to the field of Parental Alienation. EPAP works towards offering a central reference point for the dissemination of best practices and research in Parental Alienation through our first ever European accredited training course in Parental Alienation Studies and through the publication of the European Journal of Parental Alienation Practice.

Challenges:

1 To distill the complexity of what EPAP stands for into a concise design that communicates the professionalism, academic riguer and trustworthiness that EPAP wants to embody.


2 To integrate colour within the design and create an approachable, yet memorable, brand, keeping in mind EPAP's field of operation.


3 To include the journal component within the design while staying concise, especially given how hefty the titles already are.
The reference points given at the outset of this project were a set of keywords and the logo used by Parental Alienation Europe.


I chose to focus on the three keywords highlighted: co-parenting, split and loyalties. A search for the phrase “Parental Alienation” yielded the selection of images on the right. The images give a general sense of how other people have visually distilled the concept of Parental Alienation, and affirmed the chosen keywords.





Building on the reference images, the starting point was a visual of splitting parents centering around a child. We floated the idea of doing this symbolically, by using a universal reference for children. it was suggested that a Teddy Bear could be used as a unisex object.

The teddy bear did not work well—its silhouette is too ambiguous and in this context it did not yield the desired effect. However, the image of parental split worked well and I decided to develop this further.








To heighten the split I elongated the silhouettes, which created the possibility of accentuating a direction. To replace the teddy bear, I simply made a child's head, which wroked great.

This design felt quite dynamic and highlights the keywords well. There is an emphasis on splitting, on loyalties which can be emphasised by the distance between the parents and the the child and on co-parenting, when arranged to look towards the same direction. My next step was to incorporate the EPAP name with this design.

Selecting the right typeface came down to choosing between the sans-serif typeface Gotham Narrow and an manually-elongated serif typeface Xahn Mono. In the end, a sans-serif typeface complemented the design better, and a boldened Gotham Narrow fit it well with the chosen layout. Gotham Narrow is quite fascinating with its soft monolithic look, and provides an immediate and memorable look, with great legibility.

The decision to use this layout was due to how well it framed the visual emblem with the type, and helped keep it concise and compact. (I was also inspired by Bertold Löffler’s iconic Secession designs.)
By incorporating colour into the design, I wanted to add an emotional context and make the design more approachable and memorable. I chose a lighter Iron Blue and a Red Mull; contrasting to accent the difference between the parents and the child.

For the Journal version of the logo, I added a stylised 'journal' to the existing logo. This solution was a way to maintain the same identity and incorporate the Journal element and adhere to the goal of being concise.
I also also created a ‘dark mode’ version, which I thought would help the brand be more dynamic when it comes to different placements.
In the end, despite really liking the design, I felt insecure about presenting this bold (and probably unusual) design, so I decided to also include what I think is a more orthodox alternartive. This design is more compact and I think would have better legibility in different contexts.
Branding for European Parental Alienation Practice, placed on letterhead and envelope.
One of the issues I had was a lack of context as to how and where the logo will be used, except for the journal (even then I don't know if it will be published in print or digitally). The assumption is that this is primarily for digital use. Nonetheless, I made the conservative choice of placing the logos on a letterhead and envelope combo.
As this design hasn't yet been presented (as of July 2022), I expect there to be some changes to the life the logo will take and to the logo itself before being finalised.
